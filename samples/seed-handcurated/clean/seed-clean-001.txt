The B-tree data structure was first described by Bayer and McCreight in 1972. Each internal node holds a sorted set of keys and pointers to child nodes, ensuring O(log n) lookup, insertion, and deletion in the worst case. Modern filesystems and relational databases — including ext4, NTFS, PostgreSQL, and SQLite — rely on B-tree variants for index storage. The variant most commonly used in databases, the B+ tree, stores all values in leaf nodes and links the leaves into a sorted list, which makes range scans efficient at the cost of slightly larger interior nodes.
